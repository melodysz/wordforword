The first time a patient told me her whole story, I realized I had been trained to listen for symptoms, not for her. There is a particular kind of silence that opens up in a hospital room when someone stops reciting their chart and starts telling you what actually happened to them.

*Medicine teaches us to extract.* We are taught to pull the relevant detail out of a sentence the way you might pull a splinter — quickly, precisely, and without lingering on the surrounding tissue. But a person's story is not a splinter. It does not come out clean.

> "I just wanted someone to ask me how I was doing, not just what hurt."

That line has stayed with me longer than almost anything else I learned that year. It wasn't in a textbook. It was in a hallway, at 11pm, from someone who had been asked a hundred clinical questions and exactly zero human ones.

**What follows is not a solution.** It's a small collection of moments where listening, rather than diagnosing, was the entire intervention — and what changed because of it. I don't think we can teach empathy from a slide deck. I think we can only practice it, badly at first, in the room, with the people who are trusting us with their whole story instead of just their chart.
